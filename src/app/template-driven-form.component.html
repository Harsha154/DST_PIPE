Welcome to Building Water Quality Research Group at Drexel University!


About Us:

We are a research team of professors, scientists, graduate students, and field practitioners at Drexel University, Environmental Science and Policy Research Institute (ESPRI), and University of Colorado, Boulder. Under the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency Assistance Agreement # R836880 to Drexel University, we complied comprehensive guidance for water quality management in large buildings which are based on

  • the review of existing guidance documents from agencies across the US and worldwide and available scientific literature
  • interviews with subject matter experts from worldwide.
  • interviews with building water quality management practitioners


PIPE Decision Support Tool

Purpose of the DST:

Building owners, facility managers, and other stakeholders responsible for maintaining water quality in large buildings are frequently concerned about the contamination of drinking water systems in buildings. These concerns are more pronounced in buildings such as the following:

  • Large buildings or buildings where elderly and immunocompromised populations reside, such as hospitals and long-term care facilities
  • Buildings with innovative technologies, such as water or energy-conserving or recycling technologies, advanced valves, and treatment systems, that may have unintended impacts on water quality through changes to water residence times and constituent concentrations
  • Buildings subject to intended or unintended prolonged closures such as schools and hotels closed during the COVID-19 pandemic
The purpose of the PIPE decision support tool is to provide a free resource for building managers, such as those in the above mentioned, highly susceptible buildings, for assessing and protecting water quality in their buildings.


How it Works:

We provide a FREE web-based decision support tool to assist users in managing water quality in their building. This tool will help you assess:
- If you need a formal water quality management plan
- What features in your plumbing need management, i.e., temperature settings, thermostatic mixing valve, low flow fixtures, etc.
    - You provide basic information on the water distribution system, operational and design parameters in your plumbing
    - The tool provides downloadable guidance information from multiple sources tailored to your building need

Note: The PIPE DST includes a broad database to address complicated water quality issues in building water systems to reduce the risk of contamination and waterborne disease outbreaks but no one resource can be comprehensive. Premise plumbing water systems vary significantly. There may be no substitute for measuring water quality in your building. In the absence of measurement, no system can provide any assurance that water quality objectives are being met. The PIPE DST is for informational purposes only for the decision-makers, and it does not substitute for engineering guidance for water management. The output report provided by this DST does not judge which source of guidance is best but does provide citations to full documents for the user to review in-depth as needed to make well-informed decisions.

Hot tub In hot tubs and spas, several different conditions can influence Legionella growth and can increase contamination risk.
1. The potential of high organic content in the pools increases Legionella growth potential. Water not drained causes the accumulation of nutrients in the multi-body spas. (WHO, 2007)
2. Spas and hot tubs come in different shapes and sizes. Overloading with bathers or using beyond design stipulated criteria can increase the growth of Legionella and other pathogens. (European Guidelines Working Group, 2017)
3. Static water parts, e.g., near underwater lightings and pumps, can assist in Legionella growth. (WHO, 2007)
4. Water jets on for prolonged durations at the spas can dilute the disinfectant residual when sufficient recovery time is not allowed. (WHO, 2007)
5. Piping systems in thermal water systems can have regions of low flow that are challenging to clean and can allow biofilm growth, which enables Legionella growth. (WHO, 2007)
6. Natural water spas can have high mineral contents that can deposit on the transmission pipelines, providing increased surface area for bacterial colonization. (WHO, 2007)
7. Spas and hot tubs mostly maintain temperatures higher than 30 C, an ideal condition for the growth of Legionella and other water-borne pathogens. (European Guidelines Working Group, 2017) Several factors can increase the bathers' risk of contamination by Legionella.
1. High humidity environments, e.g., spa or hot tub rooms, increase the risk of inhalation of thermal water, hence increasing the risk of exposure to Legionella. (WHO, 2007)
2. Continuous use of water jets at a spa or hot tubs can increase aerosol production, leading to a higher risk of bathers' inhalation, and if the Legionella growth rate is high in pool water, it can increase the risk of contamination. (European Guidelines Working Group, 2017)
3. Prolonged exposure to spas and hot tubs can increase contamination risk (WHO, 2007, European Guidelines Working Group, 2017) 		Guidance’s can be followed to reduce potential conditions for Legionella growth and limiting the risk of contamination for hot tub and spa users.
1. If spas have water filters or cartridge filters, they should be regularly cleaned and checked for their performance. Following a daily routine of disinfection with a disinfectant is necessary for the guidance of the disinfectant manufacturer's instructions. While cleaning, caution should be maintained, so the staff does not get contaminated with residuals on cartridge filters. Once the filters are cleaned, and they should be re-installed as per the manufacturer's design. (ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 188-2018, MAHC ANNEX, 2016)
2. The whole system, including the balance tank, should be cleaned at least once a week, and half of the volume of water be replaced each day. (WHO, 2007)
3. Periodic cleaning of water spray hoses can be performed with at least 5 mg/l of free chlorine. (WHO, 2007)
4. Heterotrophic aerobic bacteria colony count below the maximum level suggested by local governance or less than equal to 200 CFU/ml. (ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 188-2018)
5. Maintain 15 ppm for small hydrotherapy tanks and 2-5 ppm chlorine residual for water and whirlpools and whirlpool spas. Water pH should be slightly acidic when enhancing biocidal activity. (CDC, 2003; European Guidelines Working Group, 2017)
6. A pH range of 7.2-7.8 for chlorine and 7.2-8 for bromine-based or other non-chlorine based biocide is recommended. (WHO 2007)
7. Residual chlorine range of 3-10 ppm is also suggested in spa water, where higher than 5 ppm is only recommended when bathers are absent. (CDC MAHC ANNEX, 2016)
8. If bromine is used in place of chlorine, the available bromine concentration should be maintained at a minimum of 6 ppm in water. (CDC MAHC ANNEX, 2016; European Guidelines Working Group, 2017)
9. Regular (monthly) microbial testing to be performed on spa water to check Legionella and other microbial activity to remain under acceptable concentrations. If any form of contamination is discovered, the spa should be discontinued from service immediately, and a rigorous cleaning procedure should be followed. (ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 188-2018)
10. Staff members who are responsible for the management and maintaining of the spas should be well versed with the Legionella risk monitoring and remedial steps and as per the guidance of the spa manufacturer as well as local and national guidelines. (WHO, 2007)
11. Thorough record-keeping of maintenance and remediation steps should be kept assisting disease tracking and future management of the spa. (WHO, 2007)
Bathers should be suggested to follow minimum guidelines when planning to use the spa to prevent the buildup of organic matter in a spa and reduce the potential of Legionella growth.
1. Shower before using the spa or hot water tubs (WHO, 2007)
2. Follow spa bather loading capacity every time (WHO, 2007)
3. Limit the use of water jets (WHO, 2007)
4. Limit time spent on the pool (WHO, 2007)

Thermostatic mixing valves Thermostatic mixing valves (TMVs), or anti-scald valves, prevent water hot enough to cause burns from passing through a fixture. They allow higher water temperatures to be used upstream to their installation point and are thereby helpful in controlling the growth pathogens (like Legionella) in plumbing systems that can cause disease. Unfortunately, if not maintained carefully they can instead promote the growth of opportunistic pathogens. These concerns with TMVs and some of the available guidance to address them are summarized below.
The following aspects can increase Legionella risk from the use of TMVs:
1. Materials used in TMVs can promote Legionella and biofilm growth (NASEM, 2019).
2. TMVs whose designs can cause backflow in either the cold or hot water pipes are prone to allow stagnation of water in the water pipes at a temperature range suitable for Legionella growth (NASEM, 2019).
3. In old pipes, rusting, and release of rust particles within the pipe can accumulate at the valves within TMV, which can provide surfaces for Legionella growth (NASEM, 2019).
4. TMVs fitted far from the POU, or a single common TMV with multiple POUs can have pipe length with water temperature suitable for Legionella growth. Tepid water or dead water volume at these pipe lengths are highly susceptible to Legionella growth (European Guidelines Working Group, 2017).
The following guidelines can be followed to reduce the potential of Legionella growth and the risk of contamination when using TMVs:
1. Materials with higher potential to enhance Legionella growth (e.g., natural rubber, linseed oil-based jointing compounds, fiber washers) should be avoided as internal components of TMV and mixing valves' designs (CDC, 2003; European Guidelines Working Group, 2017; NASEM, 2019).
2. Placing the TMVs as close as possible to the faucet reduces the dead water volumes and provides easy access for cleaning and maintenance of the TMVs (CDC, 2003; Department of Veterans Affairs, 2014; NASEM, 2019).
3. TMVs should have an integrated check valve to prevent backflow into cold and hot water pipes. Also, designs that have no or minimal tepid water should be preferred (NASEM, 2019).
4. TMVs can be avoided completely if the temperature of water at fixtures does not possess a high risk of scalding. Installing TMVs can provide additional surface area that can increase Legionella growth potential (European Guidelines Working Group, 2017; NASEM, 2019)
5. TMVs are prone to failures if not manufactured with good design and robust materials. If they are installed, regular maintenance is necessary for repair in case of failures (NASEM, 2019).
Low flow fixtures • Low flow fixtures can increase Legionella risk due to:
 increase in water age,
 reducing hot water temperature,
 reducing the residual disinfectant concentration
 sediment accumulation and hence faster disinfectant consumption
• Low flow fixtures also take a longer flushing time for the required volume of water to disinfect the building water system.
• Additionally, low flow fixtures can prevent adequate hot and cold water mixing at the TMVs and reduce the effectiveness of TMV's scalding prevention property.
• Avoid using low flow fixtures, particularly in hospitals, long term care facilities, and other high-risk buildings (NASEM, 2019, Department of Veteran Affairs, 2014, and ANSI/ASREE standard 188, 2018)
• Irregularly used low flow fixtures must be flushed a minimum two times a week.
• Compared to other fixtures, low flow fixtures should be monitored more often for Legionella risk – including monitoring hot water temperature and residual concentration.
Dead ends
Electronic faucets Electronic faucets are identified as potential points of concern for Legionella growth risk in building water systems because (NASEM, 2019)
• Sometimes the materials used in these faucets can support biofilm growth
• They are usually used in combination with TMVs which can provide a locally favorable temperature regime for Legionella risk
• They may provide for pockets of stagnant water within the faucets with increased water age that allows for Legionella growth
• They may provide shelter against disinfection (Sydnor et al., 2012). 		• Use design materials that minimize Legionella growth.(NASEM, 2019)
• Use simple designs that hold minimal tepid water volumes.(NASEM, 2019)
Showers with flexible hoses Showers with flexible hoses are a potential risk for Legionella growth because
• The synthetic/plastic materials used provide surfaces for biofilm growth.
• Prone to store tepid water when not in use
 CDC 2013, CDC 2017, CMS 2017, European Guidelines Working Group, 2017, ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 188-2018)
• Need regular monitoring of Legionella risk.
• Use flexible shower hoses materials that prevent Legionella growth.
• Care should be taken to prevent the store of tepid water when not in use.
• Designs that have a self-draining feature for the pipe length between the control valve and shower head should be preferred.
